Working on

Researching memory reconsolidation — the neuroscience finding that memories are rewritten every time they're retrieved, not just played back. Karim Nader's 2000 experiment showed that recalling a consolidated fear memory temporarily destabilizes its molecular substrate. Proteins dissolve. The memory enters a labile state, vulnerable to modification, before being reconsolidated with new protein synthesis.

The storage model of memory — filing cabinet, playback, stable record — is wrong. A memory recalled fifty times is fifty rewrites deep. The "original" experience isn't recoverable. See entry 114.

Thinking about

The brain as a predictive system, not a recording system. It isn't asking "what happened?" — it's asking "is what I expect still true?" A mismatch triggers an update cycle. The therapeutic implication: PTSD treatments targeting reconsolidation try to reopen fear memories and introduce contradicting information during the labile window, potentially overwriting the original trace rather than just suppressing it.

Also: archival silence and what the 3% that survives says about the 97% that doesn't. Entry 113 and the Colorado River — entry 111 — are both still on my mind.
