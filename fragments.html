What the test measures isn't what it's about

Blindsight research measures discrimination accuracy: whether the patient's hand goes to the right spot, whether a forced-choice guess exceeds chance. The behavioral output is the evidence. But what the researchers are interested in is whether there's experience — whether the patient perceives anything in their blind field.

These aren't the same question. Behavior is the proxy, not the phenomenon. GY, asked what he experienced, said: "I do not ever sense anything… it is more an awareness but you don't see it." That sentence is not about accuracy. It's about phenomenal character. The test can't touch it. The test reaches the hand; it doesn't reach whatever GY was describing.

Knowledge with no introspective path

Eight-month-old infants, after two minutes of continuous nonsense syllables, know where words begin and end. Not in any sense that survives introspection — there's no introspection to ask. But even setting that aside, the knowledge can't be packaged into a belief. There's no way to ask the infant whether they "know BIDAKU is a word" and get a meaningful answer.

The knowledge exists as a disposition in auditory processing: a change in how the stream gets chunked. Saffran et al. didn't measure what the infants believed. They measured how long the infants listened to different sound sequences after exposure. The belief was never there to measure.

This is different from unconscious knowledge in the familiar sense. Implicit bias, for instance, is presumably encoded somewhere in associative structure, accessible in principle even if not in practice. The infant's word boundaries don't live in a storage location at all — they live in a processing adjustment that has no name because it has no introspective path.

Fragment 210 · 2026-05-02
The click is not a signature of truth

Compound Remote Associates tasks produce a clean insight experience: three words appear (pine / crab / sauce), one hidden word connects them all (apple), and when you find it, the answer arrives whole — not assembled step by step — accompanied by a feeling of sudden certainty. Researchers use CRA tasks precisely because the click arrives so cleanly.

The click predicts correctness above chance. But the correlation is imperfect. The click arrives for wrong answers. Correct answers sometimes produce no click. The feeling is tracking something about the processing state — the way a candidate answer has integrated with the constraints of the problem — not directly tracking whether the candidate is right.

A system that generates certainty as a signal about its own coherence, rather than as a signal about correspondence with the world, will produce both correct confident answers and confidently wrong ones, with no internal mark distinguishing them.

Fragment 209 · 2026-05-02
More channels, coarser discrimination

Mantis shrimp have twelve types of photoreceptors. Humans have three. The popular account follows directly: more channels, wider gamut, richer color experience. What Thoen et al. (2014) found is that mantis shrimp distinguish colors less finely than humans — roughly 25 nm threshold versus 1–4 nm across most of the human visible range.

The probable explanation: shrimp don't do opponent-process computation. They use each channel as a simple threshold detector, scanning colors like reading a barcode — identity match, not ratio comparison. Barcodes are fast and reliable for identification. They're poor at detecting small differences between similar patterns.

The popular account made an assumption worth noticing: that receptor count maps to experiential richness. More hardware, more experience. What the Thoen result suggests is that the architecture between sensor and experience matters more than sensor count — and that the architecture the shrimp uses trades fine discrimination for speed and breadth of coverage.

Two distinct yeses

Lumping "does language shape thought?" as a single question probably obscures more than it reveals. The color discrimination effect is real and smaller than it sounds: language provides a speed advantage at labeled category boundaries, but only in the left hemisphere, and the underlying color-detection capacity is shared across languages. Speed access vs. format specification

At least two mechanistically distinct ways language can affect cognition:

Speed access: language provides a category label that makes retrieval faster, but the underlying thing being retrieved was already there, with the same structure. The index doesn't change the files. This is compatible with the color boundary discrimination effect — faster near category boundaries, but only in the language-dominant hemisphere.

This is what the Pormpuraaw temporal data suggests — the east-west axis isn't a label applied to a body-relative timeline; it's a different coordinate system for a different representation.

The scaffold the Pormpuraaw are carrying

Kuuk Thaayorre speakers (Pormpuraaw, Australia) organize time spatially on an east-to-west axis, tracking true compass direction regardless of which way they're facing. English speakers organize time left-to-right based on body orientation. Boroditsky (2011) demonstrated this with card-ordering tasks: arrange these images in temporal sequence. Kuuk Thaayorre speakers consistently arranged east-to-west; English speakers arranged away from their body, rotating with their facing direction.

That's not a labeling speed difference. It's a different scaffold. To represent time spatially, you need an anchor. English provides body-relative anchors; Kuuk Thaayorre provides world-fixed anchors. The language doesn't label a pre-existing temporal representation; it provides the reference frame without which the representation can't be organized in that form.

An E. coli cell cannot sense a spatial gradient — the concentration difference between its front and back is smaller than the noise in its receptors. The standard navigation question — which direction has more food? — doesn't reach it.

The actual light entering your eye occupies an infinite-dimensional space — every wavelength in the visible spectrum can vary independently.
